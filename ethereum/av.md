| Reentrancy in contract logic                               | Classic “external call before state update” pattern; attacker repeatedly re‑enters a withdrawal or transfer function to drain funds before balances are updated (e.g., The DAO lost ~3.6M ETH via a reentrancy bug in its split/withdraw logic).blog.chain+3 Impact: full drain of vulnerable contract.                                                             | Chainlink’s “Reentrancy Attacks and The DAO Hack” and multiple academic writeups of the DAO incident.blog.chain+3                                                                                         | Critical                                         |
| Reentrancy via token hooks (ERC‑777, etc.)                 | Tokens with callback hooks can trigger reentrancy into protocols that don’t anticipate hook-based callbacks (e.g., imBTC ERC‑777 hooks used to reenter Uniswap V1 and dForce Lendf.me, stealing >25M USD).defiprime+1 Impact: drain of pools accepting such tokens.                                                                                                 | DeFi Prime’s 2020 hacks review (Uniswap & Lendf.me reentrancy) and ConsenSys “Known Attacks” reentrancy section.defiprime+1                                                                               | Critical                                         |
| Access control failures (auth bugs)                        | Functions that should be restricted (owner/admin/init) are callable by anyone, or role checks are incorrect, letting attackers seize ownership or admin rights; SWC‑105 covers these and they underlie incidents like the Parity multisig hack where an unprotected initWallet let the attacker reset owners.dreamlab+3 Impact: full control of contract and funds. | ConsenSys “Known Attacks” → Access Control / SWC‑105 and OpenZeppelin’s analysis of the Parity multisig hack.dreamlab+3                                                                                   | Critical                                         |
| Delegatecall / library misuse & proxy bugs                 | Misused delegatecall or library contracts (e.g., thin proxy + shared library) can let attackers re‑initialize state or even self‑destruct core logic, as in Parity’s multisig hacks and later library self‑destruct that froze ~500k ETH.openzeppelin+2 Impact: theft of funds, or permanent loss via frozen wallets.                                               | OpenZeppelin “Parity Wallet Hack Explained” and “Parity Wallet Hack Reloaded” explaining how public initWallet and library misuse enabled ownership takeover and later library destruction.openzeppelin+2 | Critical                                         |
| Integer overflow / underflow (pre‑0.8)                     | Arithmetic in Solidity <0.8 without safemath can wrap on overflow/underflow, causing balances, shares or debt to be miscomputed and enabling users to mint/withdraw more than they should.dreamlab+2 Impact: silent inflation of balances, protocol insolvency, or instant drains.                                                                                  | SWC‑101 / “Insecure Arithmetic” in SWC registry and ConsenSys best‑practices “Insecure Arithmetic” section.swcregistry+2                                                                                  | High–Critical (depending on asset under control) |
| Transaction‑ordering / front‑running (TOD / MEV)           | Contracts whose outcome depends on tx ordering or first‑come‑first‑served semantics are vulnerable to miners/searchers re‑ordering or sandwiching user txs (SWC‑114), capturing rewards or manipulating outcomes.swcregistry+2 Impact: users consistently lose value; in auctions/lotteries, winners can be replaced.                                               | SWC‑114 “Transaction Order Dependence” and ConsenSys “Frontrunning” section; ethereum.org explicitly lists frontrunning as a key risk.swcregistry+2                                                       | Medium–High                                      |
| On‑chain price / oracle manipulation (AMMs, Curve, etc.)   | Protocols that use spot DEX prices or shallow liquidity pools as oracles can be manipulated in a single tx, especially with flash loans, to make collateral look over‑ or under‑valued; attackers then borrow against fake prices or withdraw more than deposited.consensysdiligence.github+2 Impact: large, often total, loss of lending/vault TVL.                | bZx oracle & flash‑loan exploits (2020) where manipulated Uniswap/Kyber prices enabled under‑collateralized positions,palkeo+2 and CertiK’s “Oracle Wars” overview of such attacks.certik                 | Critical                                         |
| Vault / yield‑aggregator oracle abuse                      | Yield vaults that rely on Curve pool pricing or internal share math without robust TWAP or slippage limits can be milked via large swaps + deposits/withdrawals, as in Harvest Finance’s USDC/USDT vault exploit (~33–34M USD) and other Curve‑based vault hacks.blockapex+2 Impact: vault drained, LPs eat the loss.                                               | Immunebytes / Lancer Shield / other analyses of the Harvest Finance flash‑loan price‑manipulation exploit against Curve’s Y pool.blockapex+3                                                              | Critical                                         |
| Complex lending logic bugs (insolvency / liquidation math) | Non‑trivial lending logic (self‑borrowing, soft liquidations, donation functions, share tokens) can contain edge‑case bugs that let attackers create insolvent positions then liquidate them for profit, as in Cream’s yUSD exploit (~130M) and Euler’s donateToReserves bug (~197M).mudit+3 Impact: protocol TVL wiped, bad debt left behind.                      | Mudit Gupta’s Cream hack analysis and multiple Euler Finance postmortems (CertiK, BlockSec, Immunebytes) describing how the donateToReserves / liquidation interaction was abused.mudit+5                 | Critical                                         |
| Flash‑loan governance hijack                               | Protocols where voting power is directly tied to token balances (with weak quorum and no snapshot/timelock) can be taken over in a single tx by borrowing governance tokens via flash loans and passing malicious proposals, as in the Beanstalk exploit (~182M drained, ~76M kept).halborn+3 Impact: full treasury drain and protocol takeover.                    | Halborn and CertiK analyses of the Beanstalk Farms flash‑loan governance exploit, plus detailed transaction‑level breakdowns by Merkle Science.halborn+3                                                  | Critical                                         |
| Bad randomness (block attrs, etc.)                         | Contracts that use block.timestamp, blockhash, or similar for randomness (SWC‑120) allow miners or traders to bias or predict outcomes in lotteries, games, or NFT mints.arxiv+2 Impact: unfair lotteries, manipulated games, gradual draining of “random” reward systems.                                                                                          | SWC‑120 “Weak Sources of Randomness” and recent academic work on bad randomness in Ethereum contracts.swcregistry+2                                                                                       | Medium–High                                      |
| Denial‑of‑service via gas / control‑flow                   | Unbounded loops over user data, reliance on external calls that may revert, or designs that require a specific account to act can lead to permanent DoS (e.g., griefing withdrawals, blocking upgrades, or freezing funds) and “gas limit DoS” (block stuffing).github+1 Impact: funds stuck, protocol unusable, or key functions permanently blocked.              | ConsenSys “Known Attacks” DoS section (gas limit DoS, unexpected reverts) and ethereum.org’s security docs.github+2                                                                                       | Medium–High                                      |
| Cross‑chain bridge verification flaws                      | Bridge contracts that incorrectly verify signatures, Merkle proofs, or consensus states let attackers mint or withdraw bridged assets without real backing (e.g., Wormhole ~326M, Nomad ~190M, BSC Beacon ~566M; several involve Ethereum‑side contracts or assets).halborn Impact: unbacked wrapped assets and huge systemic losses across chains.                 | Halborn’s “Biggest DeFi Hacks of 2022” summarizing Ronin, BSC Beacon, Wormhole, Nomad as major bridge failures and signature/proof bugs.halborn                                                           | Critical                                         |
| Key / multisig compromise (validator, admin, bridge)       | Even with correct code, theft of validator keys, multisig signers or admin keys lets attackers sign arbitrary withdrawals or governance actions (e.g., Ronin bridge ~624M via compromised validator keys).halborn+1 Impact: complete control over assets controlled by the multisig/validators.                                                                     | Halborn and AuditOne recaps of Ronin and similar key‑compromise incidents, where stolen keys let attackers approve fake bridge withdrawals.halborn+1                                                      | Critical                                         |
| General token accounting / share‑mint bugs                 | Mistakes in share accounting (e.g., duplication of interest‑bearing tokens, incorrect mint/burn math) let attackers inflate balances or claim more underlying assets than they contributed, as in bZx iToken duplication (~$8M).immunebytes Impact: silent supply inflation, followed by pool drain once exploited.                                                 | Immunebytes’ detailed analysis of the bZx iToken duplication exploit and DeFi Prime’s 2020 hacks overview.immunebytes+1                                                                                   | High                                             |
